White supremacist ideology has sought to snuff out native American cultures, like an oppressive blanket over a bright fire. But now, the pressure is as great as ever, with more pieces of human history being lost to the past every day. Language is a key cultural trait that uses symbology to give meaning and a sense of belonging, with huge cultural significance. All over America, time is cruelly claiming the lives of the last speakers of a variety of languages, leaving only the vague memories of the once vivacious words on the tips of people's tongues.

Since times of colonization, indigenous people have had their culture under attack due to the desecration of their culture that resulted from Indian residence schools. According to American Indian Boarding Schools Haunt Many, by Chana Bear, “Wright [student as a residence school] remembers matrons bathing him in kerosene and shaving his head. Students at federal boarding schools were forbidden to express their culture-everything from wearing long hair to speaking even a single Indian word.” Without being able to express or practice their culture, many aspects of Native American culture were not passed on to the younger generations. In order to keep a culture

culture alive the most important thing is to pass it down to the next generation. Traditions cannot be continually practiced if they are unknown. When these horrifying schools were finally put to a stop, Native American children found themselves no longer understanding their families. A language barrier was formed among families and dozens of languages were put in grave danger.

In this past decade, the quantity of speakers of an indigenous language has been declining at a rate of 6%. Most languages only have a couple thousand of speakers left, and some have less than 50. To understand why this is truly happening, we have to look at how English has begun to engross every corner of daily life for people all over the world. Although the British colonial forces largely contributed to the establishment of English as a lingua franca, or a universal language, in recent years there has been another factor that has served as a catalyst: social media. Pop culture is a backbone in American culture and therefore, had been the main producer of content on social media. From influencers on TikTok to Youtubers, English is the language you will most commonly see pop-culture media in. It has diffused contagiously along with social media, leading to a risk of developing a cultural homogenization.Consultant Zalmai Zanir says, “If we don't keep those structures [referring to language immersion home] going, the competition with English is too great”. The article Language Immersion House helps Revitalize Lushootseed by Molly Bryant is about a home in Washington where people come to speak, eat, and sleep in Lushootseed, the language of the Coast Salish people who are native to the Seattle area. Throughout the piece, it is stated that a language next, a physical language where you do not allow English to contaminate, is necessary to revive the dying language. English has seeped into too many parts of daily life, replacing people's native tongues as it seems the easier way out. If this continues, soon more than languages will be lost to Western culture.

What is cultural homogenization? It is defined as the process where local or distinct cultures become more similar or uniform due to the influence of a dominant culture, often leading to a decline in cultural diversity, and it is something that folk cultures and Native American cultures have always been against. Native American cultures value diversity and distinctness of each tribe that belongs to them. All their traditions and their languages are deeply rooted to who they are as a culture, with every little detail having significance and symbolism, and cultural homogenization puts all of that at risk. Cultural homogenization is said to occur through globalization, media, and technology, where a dominant culture's practices, values, and products are adopted, potentially overshadowing unique local traditions. As stated in The Language of the Land, “Centuries of genocide, disease, and forced assimilation policies took their toll on the number of first language speakers,” which shows how efforts to inflict cultural homogenization on Native American cultures has been going on for far longer than most believe.

The contagious diffusion, a type of expansion diffusion, of English as a lingua franca (a common language that allows people from diverse linguistic backgrounds to understand each other) through social media has had an undeniable effect on the speakers of a Native American language when looking at statistics. But in order to fully understand English’s impact, we have to take a look at English’s influence on the world. By the criteria of Wallerstein’s World System Theory, the United States qualifies as a core country, and with a GDP, or gross domestic product, of 27.72 trillion USD (2023), it’s clear it checks the significant influence in global trade and finance box in the qualifications of core countries. Due to this influence, it has been able to seep into every aspect of life, leaving its mark. How Native North American Language Use Changed in the United States by Adrienne Griffiths and Daniela Mejia reports that Native North American language use declined “20,000 speakers from 2013 to 2021,” and it must be taken into account that there is little over 300,000 speakers in the US. This means that almost an entire 10% of speakers have been lost in roughly the past decade. Beyond just its influence, English is also significantly easier to access. Due to the amount of countries that the English colonized as well as the media, it’s somewhat easy to learn English simply because of exposure. However, as it was put in the article: The Language of the Land, “There’s no conventional textbook. Instead, Honn scours many sources-folk stories, recordings, research-for relevant grammar and vocabulary,” when discussing how Native American languages are sometimes taught. It’s clear the Native American languages are having trouble competing with the ubiquity of the English language.

A Solution

Though the case may sound hopeless, it will only be hopeless if nobody takes action, which is why we have to be inspired by the efforts that have been put forward to save Native American languages, in order to bring them back to life. Perhaps the most successful solution was written about by Molly Bryant, in her article: Language Immersion House helps Revitalize Lushootseed. With the efforts that not only the Immersion House put in, but the those in the community that they inspired, the number of Lushootseed speakers “has grown from 5 in 2014 to 472 in 2022,” marking a 9340% increase in speakers, which is unheard of. Their success inspired the plan to battle the loss of Native American languages and cultural homogenization. Looking at the roots of the problem, it can be seen that younger generations aren’t learning the languages due to the influence English has had on their lives and the amount of native speakers that are passing away. The languages are being offered at colleges, such as the University of Washington, but it doesn’t seem to be working as 2 languages are still going extinct per month. That’s why the creation of a new program is needed: S.A.L.L. which stands for Saving A Language’s Life. This program would be dedicated to raising funds to instate immersion homes as a part of any Native American Language curriculum offered at universities, as well as raising funds to instate Native American Language courses in universities across the United States.

To fully understand the solution, it is necessary to outline the steps in creating the S.A.L.L language immersion homes. Step 1: Finding a group to stay in the home is necessary which starts by having a course offered at the college of the state/area. Step 2: Holding fundraisers at universities to pay for the AirBnB to be rented as the language immersion house. Step 3: Transforming the environment by labeling all items in the household with the translation of the language in which you are immersing. Step 4: Cutting out English by encouraging only the language into which students will be immersing to be spoken. Step 5: Setting a designated block of time everyday to practice conversing in the language with the group as well as reading and writing. This process would be carried out over the course of a week, and would serve as a field trip that's part of the course that students take. The program will be a non-profit that would begin right here in Seattle, as the University of Washington is already passionate about improving their Lushootseed program, with Professor Hohn being an advocate for the protecting indigenous languages. From the University of Washington, the first milestone, the program would grow and try to expand to all colleges in Washington State to reach its second milestone. From Washington State, as the program’s supporters and funds grow, the goal would be to expand alongside the entirety of the West Coast to complete its third milestone, and eventually, across the whole of the United States of America for its fourth and final milestone. The goal of S.A.L.L. is to successfully reintroduce indigenous languages to a younger generation, so they can continue to be passed down and the culture they carry with them can be kept alive.

Tami Hohn writes the word ‘alive’ in Southern Lushootseed- a campaign she started in response to the concept that languages die when they lose their first language speakers. “In our hearts, as Native people, we know that our language is not dead. It’s still alive and our mission is to strengthen its life.”
